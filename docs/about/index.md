# About

## QA Philosophy

I don't think of quality as a gate at the end of development. Quality is an engineering discipline that runs through the entire product lifecycle — from how a story is written to how a deployment is validated.

My job isn't to find bugs. It's to build systems that make bugs less likely to exist and easier to catch when they do.

That means being involved early: in requirements review, in architecture discussions, in how a feature is defined. By the time code reaches a QA phase, many of the most expensive bugs have already been decided into existence. Getting upstream is where the real leverage is.

---

## Engineering Mindset

I approach testing the same way I approach any engineering problem — start from what matters, then work backward to what needs to be verified.

Not everything deserves the same depth of coverage. A payment flow and a settings page are not the same risk. I allocate test effort based on failure impact and probability, not feature count or gut feel.

I'm also pragmatic about automation. Automation is an investment, not a checkbox. Before writing a test, I ask whether it will actually run reliably, whether someone will maintain it, and whether the signal it provides is worth the cost. A suite of 50 stable, meaningful tests beats 500 tests that require constant babysitting.

---

## How I Work with Teams

I've worked in Agile environments with geographically distributed teams. Cross-regional collaboration has shaped how I communicate: I've learned to write clearly rather than assume shared context, to raise concerns early rather than let them surface at release time, and to make tradeoffs explicit so everyone is working from the same understanding.

I don't use authority to enforce quality. I try to make quality the path of least resistance — fast test infrastructure, clear definitions of done, shared understanding of what "good" looks like. When the environment supports quality work, quality happens naturally.

---

## Leadership Style

When I mentor junior QA engineers, I try to teach judgment more than process. Process is easy to document. Knowing when to break from process, when to push back, and when to let something go — that takes exposure and feedback.

I run reviews as conversations, not evaluations. I surface tradeoffs rather than prescribing solutions. I want the people I work with to develop their own engineering thinking, not just follow a checklist I handed them.

---

## Continuous Learning

The QA field has changed significantly in the time I've been doing this work. Mobile platforms, AI-assisted tooling, and distributed delivery have all changed what good testing looks like.

I stay current by building things — automation frameworks, test suites, tooling experiments — not just by reading about them. I've been using AI tools actively in my QA workflow and have found them genuinely useful for specific tasks (test case drafting, requirement analysis, bug investigation). I'm also clear-eyed about where they fall short.

The part I find most interesting is figuring out where AI tools change what a senior QA engineer needs to spend their time on — and what they can't replace.

---

## Why QA

I like working on problems where correctness matters and failure is expensive. Quality engineering is fundamentally about reducing the cost of being wrong — and that problem is never fully solved. There's always more interesting work to do.
