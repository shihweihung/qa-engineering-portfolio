# Cross-Functional Communication

## The Core Challenge

QA sits at the intersection of engineering and product. The most common failure mode is QA becoming a gate at the end of the process rather than a voice in the engineering conversation throughout.

I've spent a significant part of my career figuring out how to be present and credible in conversations with engineers and product managers — not just as the person who finds bugs, but as someone who contributes to quality decisions early, when they're cheaper to make.

---

## With Engineering Teams

The relationship with engineers is the most important one for QA effectiveness. Engineers who view QA as adversarial will hide problems. Engineers who view QA as a useful check will surface problems early.

What I've found builds credibility with engineers:

- **Technical substance** — Understanding the system well enough to ask specific questions about what can go wrong, rather than generic "have you tested this?" questions
- **Clear bug reports** — Reproducible steps, relevant context, specific behavior versus expected behavior. Vague bug reports waste everyone's time.
- **Proportional severity assessment** — Not every bug is a blocker. Engineers lose respect for QA that cries wolf.
- **Acknowledging uncertainty** — When I don't know whether something is a bug or expected behavior, saying so rather than filing a bug on something I haven't fully understood.

---

## With Product Teams

Product managers care about shipping. My job in those relationships is to make the risk visible so they can make an informed decision — not to block releases by treating every issue as a blocker.

I've developed the habit of framing test results as risk information rather than pass/fail verdicts. "We have two unresolved issues in the payment flow; here's their severity and the workaround status" is more useful to a PM than "we're not ready to ship."

---

## Distributed and Cross-Regional Teams

I've worked in environments where engineering, product, and QA are distributed across different time zones and cultural contexts. The communication challenges in these environments are real:

- Asynchronous communication requires more explicit documentation of reasoning than synchronous does
- Technical disagreements are harder to resolve when the parties don't share a communication style or organizational context
- Building trust takes longer when the relationship is primarily remote

What I've found works: over-documenting decisions and their rationale, being direct about disagreements rather than letting them fester asynchronously, and building personal relationships with key counterparts even when the relationship is primarily remote. The last one is underrated — a brief video call to resolve something that would take three days to resolve in email is usually worth it.
