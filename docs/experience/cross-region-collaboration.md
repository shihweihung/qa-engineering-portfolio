# Cross-Region Collaboration

## What I've Done

I've worked in a QA environment where the team is distributed across multiple regions and time zones. That means some conversations happen asynchronously, technical decisions involve people with different engineering cultures and tooling preferences, and alignment on shared practices requires explicit effort rather than casual hallway discussion.

---

## Responsibilities

**Coordinating QA efforts across teams**

When QA work is split between regional teams, you can't assume the other team is covering what you think they're covering. I've learned to make coverage explicit — who owns which test areas, what's in scope for each team's regression, and where the gaps are.

**Technical alignment on shared tooling**

Distributed teams often have independently developed tooling — different frameworks, different conventions, different opinions about the right approach. I've been involved in evaluating competing approaches and helping the team arrive at decisions that are technically sound, not just politically convenient.

**Communication across time zones and working styles**

Writing clearly matters more when you can't resolve ambiguity in real time. I've developed a habit of being more explicit in written communication than I would be face-to-face — stating assumptions, flagging open questions, and summarizing decisions in a place where both sides can refer back to them.

**Navigating disagreement without escalation**

Cross-regional disagreements sometimes have as much to do with team ownership and autonomy as with technical merit. I've learned to separate the technical question ("which framework is better suited for this problem?") from the organizational question ("who gets to build and maintain it?") — and address both, because conflating them usually means neither gets resolved well.

---

## Challenges

**Different standards and conventions**

What counts as a good test case, how bugs are documented, what "release ready" means — these vary between teams even when the teams are working toward the same product. I've had to figure out which differences are worth aligning on and which ones can coexist without causing problems.

**Decision-making without clear authority**

In cross-regional settings, decisions often require buy-in from multiple stakeholders who have different priorities. I've been in situations where the technically correct answer was clear to me but wasn't going to land unless it was framed in a way that addressed the other team's concerns. Learning to do that without compromising the technical integrity of the decision took time.

**Building trust at a distance**

Working relationships that develop naturally in an office take more deliberate effort when you're remote. I've found that being consistently reliable — saying what I'll do and doing it — builds more trust faster than any amount of meeting or messaging.

---

## Lessons Learned

Cross-regional collaboration taught me to think carefully about what I say and how I say it. Written communication is more permanent and more easily misread than spoken communication. The extra care that goes into writing clearly pays back more than the time it costs.

It also taught me that organizational dynamics are real and have to be dealt with honestly. Pretending that disagreements are purely technical when there's something else going on doesn't make the something else go away — it just makes the conversation less productive.
